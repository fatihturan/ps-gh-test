Creating a Bar Graph

Making charts in Pine is simple and intuitive. Use bar graphs when you have at least one category data (like names of quarterbacks) and numerical data for that category (like passing yards). For example, say you want to visualize quarterback passing yards, both by total yards ever thrown and by average yards thrown in a game. Using a bar chart would be perfect for this because it allows you to stack all of the QBs you want to make a comparison for, and then see the total yards they've thrown in their careers, or view the average number of yards they throw in a game.

All charts and graphs are made using the Visualize feature in the main data window. The short video below walks you through how to select QB data, open the Visualize interface, and create a graph of QB passing yard stats over the last 20 years, and the average yards they throw in a game.


Creating a Scatter Plot

Like bar graphs, scatter plots are made using the Visualize interface. Use scatter plots when you have at least two pieces of numerical data. For example, let's say you want to see how wind speed affects how many yards a quarterback throws. Here, you'd have two pieces of numerical data: wind speeds and yards thrown. You can plot these numbers on a graph, and then apply a trend line to visualize the the relationship between your data and help you make predictions.

Looking at Relationships

Numerical data have relationships. When the relationship between two numbers is very evident, we say the relationship is strong (this relationship is sometimes also called a correlation). For example, a person's height (which is a number) and a person's weight (also a number) have some relationship: someone who is short is likely to weigh less than someone who is tall. We could graph this relationship to see how age affects weight.

Pine lets you intuitively view the relationships between numbers by exploring the data over time. You can use the relationship tool to identify areas of interest in the data, and then create charts and stories based on that data.
